## Abstract

This paper studies the economic implications of petroleum subsidies in Indonesia using a Heterogeneous Agent New Keynesian (HANK) model calibrated to Indonesian economy. The results suggest that petroleum subsidy in Indonesia generates suboptimal outcomes relative to alternative policy regimes. Specifically, I find that eliminating petroleum subsidies increases output, improves welfare, and promotes a more equal distribution of income and wealth in the long run. Transfer-based policies, especially targeted transfers, further enhance welfare and equality outcomes. Despite these long run gains, this paper shows that policy reforms are associated with short-run economic costs such as output contraction, inflationary pressure, and widening fiscal deficits. As stabilisation instrument, I find that petroleum subsidy is ineffective. Although it mitigates initial price surges, it leads to larger output losses, more persistent price increases, and greater fiscal deterioration in response to oil price shocks. The petroleum subsidy also performs poorly to protect the poor from adverse oil price shocks.
